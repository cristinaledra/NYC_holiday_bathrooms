As visitors flock to NYC's big holiday attractions, few public restrooms are available to handle the crowds

 

By: Cristina Ledra     Dec. 22, 2024

 

Millions of people are estimated to visit the Bryant Park Winter Village and Rockefeller Center tree each year.

The fight for more public bathrooms in New York City has gone on for decades, but no such time is the problem more exacerbated than during the busy holiday season when millions of visitors flock to two of the city’s biggest attractions – the Rockefeller Center Christmas tree and Bryant Park’s Winter Village.

Organizers estimate that 125 million people visit the Rockefeller Center Christmas tree per year, while around three million attend Bryant Park’s Winter Village. While it would be difficult for any number of public bathrooms to accommodate that many people, there are still very few options within a five minute walk of either attraction.

In addition to the low number of bathrooms available, the hours of operation can also be an issue. By Rockefeller Center, the three options within a five minute walk are the 53rd public library, and two privately owned public spaces (POPS). The library is open until 6 p.m. six days a week and is closed on Sundays. The POPS at 645 Fifth Ave. is open every day but closes at 10 p.m., and the POPS at 575 Fifth Ave. is open every day until midnight. This leaves fewer public options in the evening hours and on Sundays, and no options after midnight.

The situation is similar by Bryant Park, where the park’s famous public restrooms close at 10 p.m. every day. The other two public restrooms within a five minute walk are located in libraries, which are not open on Sundays, and have varying closing hours depending on the day. The earliest closing time is 5 p.m. and the latest is 9 p.m.

Advocates say these facilities are not adequate for the number of residents in New York City, much less the tourists that flock to the city throughout the year.

“There are some people who think, ‘Well, if you need a bathroom, you can just go into a restaurant.’ That’s actually not true for everyone,” Alison Wilkey, the director of government affairs at Coalition for the Homeless told City Limits. “One, a lot of places require that you make a purchase, and two, there are definitely racial differences in how people are perceived when they walk in and ask for a bathroom.”

Although Mayor Eric Adams announced his “Ur In Luck” initiative in June, the 46 new public restrooms and 36 renovated restrooms in the next five years promised in the bill are not enough, Wilkey said.

“It’s great that we are building more bathrooms, but bathrooms take time to build,” Wilkey said. “The city could take an additional step right now to make more bathrooms available by opening up bathrooms in government buildings.”

As part of the Ur in Luck initiative, the city created a Google Maps layer that anyone can download to find the nearest public restroom, but at certain hours, visitors to the midtown holiday attractions might have to rely on the giving spirit of private businesses if they need to use the restroom.
